11-14 minutes | Face-to-face with examiner | Recorded interview

The IELTS General Training Speaking test is identical to the Academic version. It assesses your ability to communicate effectively in English through a structured interview with a qualified examiner. The test evaluates your spoken English across four key criteria with equal weighting.

Assessment Criteria (Equal 25% Each)

üëã Part 1: Introduction and Interview

Duration & Format

4-5 minutes | Personal questions | Familiar topics

Part 1 begins with the examiner introducing themselves and confirming your identity. You'll then answer questions about familiar topics such as your home, family, work, studies, and interests. This section assesses your ability to provide personal information and express opinions on everyday subjects.

Common Topic Categories:

üè† Personal Information Basic Details
Topics: Name, hometown, living arrangements, family, accommodation
Sample: "Tell me about your hometown. What do you like about living there?"
Strategy: Give extended answers with reasons and examples
üíº Work and Study Current Situation
Topics: Job, career plans, studies, subjects, future goals
Sample: "Do you work or are you a student? What do you enjoy about your work/studies?"
Strategy: Be specific about your experiences and aspirations
üé® Hobbies and Interests Personal Preferences
Topics: Leisure activities, sports, music, reading, travel, food
Sample: "What do you like to do in your free time? How often do you do this activity?"
Strategy: Show enthusiasm and provide detailed descriptions

Essential Part 1 Strategies

  • Extend your answers beyond simple yes/no responses
  • Provide reasons and examples to support your statements
  • Use varied vocabulary and grammatical structures
  • Stay natural and conversational while being informative
  • Don't memorize answers - be genuine and spontaneous
  • Practice common topics but be prepared for variations

üìã Part 2: Long Turn (Individual Presentation)

Duration & Format

3-4 minutes | 1 minute preparation + 1-2 minutes speaking | Cue card topic

Part 2 presents you with a cue card containing a topic and bullet points to address. You have one minute to prepare notes, then must speak for 1-2 minutes without interruption. The examiner may ask 1-2 follow-up questions. This section tests your ability to organize ideas and speak at length on a given topic.

Common Cue Card Categories:

üë§ Describe a Person People Topics
Examples: A person you admire, someone who helped you, a family member, a friend
Key points: Who they are, how you know them, their characteristics, why they're important
Language focus: Descriptive adjectives, personality traits, relationship vocabulary
üìç Describe a Place Location Topics
Examples: A place you visited, your ideal home, a memorable location, a building
Key points: Where it is, what it looks like, what you do there, why it's special
Language focus: Location prepositions, descriptive language, architectural terms
üéØ Describe an Experience Event Topics
Examples: A memorable day, a celebration, a journey, a learning experience
Key points: When and where it happened, who was involved, what occurred, how you felt
Language focus: Past tenses, sequence markers, emotional vocabulary

Essential Part 2 Strategies

  • Use the full minute to plan and make notes on key points
  • Address all bullet points mentioned on the cue card
  • Speak for the full time (aim for 1.5-2 minutes)
  • Structure your response with clear introduction, main points, and conclusion
  • Use diverse vocabulary and complex sentence structures
  • Include specific details and personal experiences
  • Practice time management to cover all points adequately

üí¨ Part 3: Two-way Discussion

Duration & Format

4-5 minutes | Abstract discussion | Follow-up to Part 2 topic

Part 3 involves a more complex discussion related to the Part 2 topic. Questions become more abstract and analytical, requiring you to express opinions, analyze issues, compare ideas, and speculate about future trends. This section assesses your ability to discuss complex topics and express sophisticated ideas.

Discussion Question Types:

üìä Compare and Contrast Analytical
Question style: "How do young and old people differ in their approach to...?"
Skills tested: Ability to identify differences and similarities
Language focus: Comparative structures, contrast markers, generalization language
üîÆ Future Speculation Predictive
Question style: "How do you think... will change in the future?"
Skills tested: Ability to predict and speculate about trends
Language focus: Future forms, probability language, conditional structures
üåç Social Issues Evaluative
Question style: "What are the advantages and disadvantages of...?"
Skills tested: Critical thinking and balanced argumentation
Language focus: Opinion markers, cause and effect, evaluation language

Essential Part 3 Strategies

  • Think before speaking - take a moment to organize your thoughts
  • Give extended responses with multiple points and examples
  • Use sophisticated language and complex grammatical structures
  • Express clear opinions while acknowledging different viewpoints
  • Support arguments with reasons, examples, and evidence
  • Engage in natural conversation - ask for clarification if needed
  • Stay flexible and be prepared to develop ideas spontaneously

üéØ Band Score Quick Reference

ü•á Band 7-9 (Expert User)

ü•à Band 5-6 (Competent User)

ü•â Band 4-5 (Modest User)
